The story in one line: the split is structural, and the weekend just widened it. The combined market cap of the top-5 services firms remains ~46% below its 2024 peak at ₹18.15 lakh crore, with most Nifty IT names still under their 200-DMA — and the relief rally that TCS's Q1 beat (profit +4.6% to ₹13,349 cr, $9.5bn order book) was supposed to trigger now has to fight a macro shock instead. Over the weekend the US struck 140 targets inside Iran, Iran fired at Gulf states and declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, and Brent ran 5.4% higher toward $79 — a direct tax on the import-heavy economy whose currency sets every offshore margin. Meanwhile global tech keeps compounding: Nasdaq +1.3% on AI-chip demand, and on July 9 three frontier labs shipped in a single day (GPT-5.6, Grok 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Pro Deep Think). The uncomfortable frame: Gartner sees $2.59tn of AI spend this year against $6.15tn of total IT spend — AI is growing at 47% while the whole budget grows at 10.8%, which means the run-the-business services layer is being cannibalised to pay for it. 166,090 tech jobs have already gone in 2026. Cloud, Infra & Chips
AI servers set to triple — the biggest line item nobody has ordered yet — Gartner expects spending on AI-optimised servers to triple over five years and become the largest single subsegment of AI spending, as cloud providers expand capacity in anticipation of GenAI and agentic workloads. That word — anticipation — is the whole risk. It's a supply-led build-out betting that the agent demand Forrester keeps failing to find in enterprises will eventually show up. Silicon · Capex
The $2.59tn vs $487bn scope trap — Gartner's $2.59 trillion counts the entire AI stack; IDC's $487bn counts AI infrastructure hardware only. They are not competing forecasts of the same thing, and one compiled analysis calls mixing them the single most common analytical error in technology budgeting. Worth knowing before someone puts both on the same slide this quarter — a 5x gap in a headline is almost always a definitional gap, not a disagreement. Analyst · Forecasts
Chips still lead the tape — Nasdaq +1.3% — Wall Street closed the week risk-on, with the Nasdaq up 1.3% as chip names rallied on renewed AI-demand optimism (S&P +0.81%, Dow +0.27%). But the weekend's escalation and a 5.4% jump in Brent toward $79 hadn't been priced when those bells rang. The semiconductor trade is the market's default risk-on position — which also makes it the most crowded thing to be holding into a Hormuz headline. Silicon · Markets
AI, GenAI & Agents
July 9: three frontier labs shipped on the same day — a first. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 arrived as a three-tier lineup (Sol at $5/$30 for top-end reasoning, Terra at GPT-5.5 quality for half the cost, Luna for cheap high-volume work), plus GPT-Live voice that listens and speaks simultaneously. Segmenting the flagship is a quiet admission that most workloads never needed it — and once buyers can see the price ladder, the fight moves from "smartest" to "cheapest sufficient." Frontier models · OpenAI
Grok 4.5's real flex is frugality — xAI's 1.5-trillion-parameter MoE, trained on Cursor interaction data, scores 83.3% on Terminal-Bench 2.1 while using about 25% of the output tokens Opus 4.8 needs on comparable tasks. Output tokens are what you're billed for, so reaching the same answer in a quarter of the words is a 4x price cut that never touches the price list. Token efficiency, not benchmark score, is the metric procurement is quietly optimising on. Frontier models · xAI
64 subagents, one hour, a 50-year-old conjecture — OpenAI reports GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra produced a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture in under an hour using 64 subagents in parallel — a problem open for roughly five decades. Peer verification is pending and matters enormously. But the number to sit with is the 64: if capability scales with compute you rent rather than intelligence you train, the bottleneck on hard problems becomes a budget line. Agents · Research

Regional risk has stopped being an abstraction in a vendor questionnaire and become a business-continuity line item. Geopolitics · Continuity
Hormuz: closed, or not, depending on who you ask — Iran declared the strait closed "until further notice" after hitting a third vessel; CENTCOM denied it and began further attacks to guarantee freedom of navigation. On July 7 the LNG tanker Al Rekayat and supertanker Wedyan were struck. A strait is functionally closed the moment underwriters believe it is — so the number that settles this argument is the war-risk premium on a Gulf hull, not a press statement. Geopolitics · Shipping
Anthropic restores Fable 5 globally, with new safeguards — Fable 5 and Mythos 5 came back worldwide on July 1 after US export controls were lifted, shipping with cybersecurity classifiers Anthropic describes as its strongest safeguards to date. It's a small item with a large implication: model availability is now an instrument of trade policy, and enterprises building on a frontier model are, whether they like it or not, exposed to export-control risk.
