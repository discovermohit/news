Cloud, Infra & Chips
AI servers set to triple — the biggest line item nobody has ordered yet — Gartner expects spending on AI-optimised servers to triple over five years and become the largest single subsegment of AI spending, as cloud providers expand capacity in anticipation of GenAI and agentic workloads. That word — anticipation — is the whole risk. It's a supply-led build-out betting that the agent demand Forrester keeps failing to find in enterprises will eventually show up. Silicon · Capex
The $2.59tn vs $487bn scope trap — Gartner's $2.59 trillion counts the entire AI stack; IDC's $487bn counts AI infrastructure hardware only. They are not competing forecasts of the same thing, and one compiled analysis calls mixing them the single most common analytical error in technology budgeting. Worth knowing before someone puts both on the same slide this quarter — a 5x gap in a headline is almost always a definitional gap, not a disagreement. Analyst · Forecasts
The chip trade keeps blinking — the pressure on semis carried into July 17: S&P −1.01% to 7,457, Dow −0.77% (−407 pts), with chipmakers sliding again. The trigger this time was, ironically, good AI news — Moonshot's Kimi K3, a near-frontier model anyone can self-host for free, reminded investors that better-and-cheaper models undercut the pricing power built into chip valuations. That's the crack that matters for services: the whole Indian AI-transformation pipeline is underwritten by hyperscaler capex plans that assume cheap, abundant compute forever. Silicon · Markets
AI, GenAI & Agents
China's Kimi K3 tops the coding arena — as Gemini 3.5 slips again — Moonshot AI's Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open MoE model with a 1M-token context, hit #1 on the Frontend Code Arena with a 76% pairwise win rate — beating Claude Fable 5 — and scored 88.3 on Terminal-Bench 2.1; full open weights land July 27. Meanwhile Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro slipped again, reportedly falling short on coding and reasoning in testing with still no model card or pricing. The irony worth sitting with: an open frontier-class model enterprises can self-host erodes both US labs' pricing power and Washington's export-control leverage at once. Frontier models · Moonshot
The $2/$10 floor expires August 31 — Claude Sonnet 5 — the model that pushed agentic coding into everyday business workflows and has been anchoring the market's price expectations all quarter — currently costs $2 per million input tokens and $10 output. From September 1 it becomes $3/$15, a 50% rise, published in advance. An introductory price with a settlement date isn't a discount, it's a land-grab; anyone whose unit economics currently pencil out at $2/$10 has about six weeks to find out whether the product survives at $3/$15. Pricing · Anthropic
The first confirmed agent-led breach — and India buys in anyway — Hugging Face disclosed an AI-agent-led intrusion, a rare confirmed case of an autonomous agent used to actually break in rather than merely assist. In the same week, LTIMindtree announced a strategic partnership with Anthropic to accelerate enterprise AI adoption, on a +17.1% profit quarter. The two are the same story: every firm racing to deploy agents is widening its own attack surface — so the integrators selling the transformation will increasingly have to sell the agent-security layer alongside it. Cybersecurity
Eight days of strikes — and now the grid is a target — the Gulf war has hardened: US forces have bombed Iran for an eighth consecutive day, the American military death toll is 16, and Trump is publicly threatening to "knock out" Iran's power plants and bridges unless Tehran negotiates. Brent jumped to $88 (+4.6% in a day, +10% on the month). That's the turn that matters for technology: the Gulf hosts US bases and a fast-growing cluster of hyperscaler data centres and sovereign-AI build-outs. Regional risk has stopped being an abstraction in a vendor questionnaire and become a business-continuity line item. Geopolitics · Continuity
Hormuz: closed, or not, depending on who you ask — Iran declared the strait closed "until further notice" after hitting a third vessel; CENTCOM denied it and began further attacks to guarantee freedom of navigation. On July 7 the LNG tanker Al Rekayat and supertanker Wedyan were struck. A strait is functionally closed the moment underwriters believe it is — so the number that settles this argument is the war-risk premium on a Gulf hull, not a press statement. Geopolitics · Shipping
Anthropic restores Fable 5 globally, with new safeguards — Fable 5 and Mythos 5 came back worldwide on July 1 after US export controls were lifted, shipping with cybersecurity classifiers Anthropic describes as its strongest safeguards to date. It's a small item with a large implication: model availability is now an instrument of trade policy, and enterprises building on a frontier model are, whether they like it or not, exposed to export-control risk. SaaS, Services & Outsourcing
167,720 gone — and now the majority blame AI — tech layoffs this year have hit 461 companies and 167,720 people, about 834 a day, with 2,115+ more in the week to July 15. The tell isn't the total, it's the reason: 54% of 2026's layoff events explicitly cite AI, automation or machine learning — 168,770 workers across 164 companies. Recent cuts: Microsoft 4,800 (Xbox worst-hit at 3,200 through FY27), Sprout Social −20%, KPMG Australia 1,000+, Thomson Reuters −500 engineers. That AI attribution is the line that turns the story from cyclical to structural. Jobs · Global tech
The AI shakeup of a $283bn sector — Reuters frames TCS's layoffs as the leading edge of an AI-driven restructuring of India's $283bn outsourcing industry — the clearest signal yet that the pyramid staffing model, not just this quarter's demand, is what's under pressure. The top-5 majors remain ~46% below their 2024 peak at ₹18.15 lakh crore. When the largest player cuts while reporting a profit beat, it isn't a downturn response; it's a redesign. India IT · Structural
TCS takes ABB's entire global network — announced jointly on July 13, a multi-million, multi-year agreement under which TCS moves beyond managing ABB's infrastructure and applications to running the Swiss engineering group's whole global network as integrated, AI-driven network-as-a-service. This is the shape of the new contract: outcome-priced, AI-delivered, and won in the same week TCS is trimming headcount. Revenue and headcount have formally decoupled at India's largest IT firm — which is precisely what Infosys will be asked about on July 23, where the Street expects +2.1% QoQ but largely on acquisitions.
