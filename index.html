Inflation turns — and the RBI's easy year just ended

Two prints in two days, and both went the wrong way. June retail inflation accelerated to 4.38% from 3.93% in May, and wholesale inflation came in at 9.68% — the highest reading in more than three years. The GST rate cuts that flattered the last few months are a fading tailwind, and crude is climbing into the gap they leave.

  • CPI 4.38% in June, up from 3.93% in May.
  • WPI at 9.68% — a three-year high, from 8.26% a month earlier.
  • Economists are now openly pricing rate-hike risk, not cuts.
  • The GST-rationalisation disinflation only holds through this quarter.
Why it matters:Wholesale inflation leads retail by a quarter or two, so a 9.68% WPI isn't a data point — it's a warning about the CPI prints still to come, which means the RBI is being handed a hawkish problem exactly as it was preparing to declare victory.

Nvidia open-sources a language model that doesn't write left to right

Every LLM you use predicts the next token, one at a time. Nvidia just published one that doesn't. Nemotron-Labs-TwoTower is an open-weight diffusion language model that generates text in parallel rather than sequentially, delivering 2.42x higher throughput while retaining 98.7% of baseline quality. A 1.3% quality haircut for a 2.4x speed-up is a trade a lot of people will take.

  • 2.42x higher throughput than autoregressive baselines.
  • Retains 98.7% of baseline quality — a near-free speed gain.
  • Open weights — anyone can run and fine-tune it.
  • Generates in parallel, not one token after another.
Why it matters:Nvidia has no reason to make inference cheaper unless cheaper inference sells more GPUs — which it does, by making previously uneconomic workloads viable, so read this less as generosity and more as the company engineering demand for its own hardware from the software layer down.

DuctGPT is inventing magnets that don't need China

This is the AI story with actual geopolitical teeth. Ames Laboratory has built a physics-trained model called DuctGPT that designs rare-earth-free permanent magnets — and unlike a generic LLM, it reasons about the underlying physics while also weighing production cost and component sourcing. Permanent magnets are the choke-hold in EV motors, wind turbines and defence hardware, and China controls the rare earths that go into them.

  • DuctGPT is physics-trained, not a general-purpose LLM.
  • Targets rare-earth-free permanent magnets.
  • Optimises for production cost and component sourcing, not just performance.
  • Built at the US Department of Energy's Ames Laboratory.
Why it matters:A model that designs around a supply constraint is doing industrial policy, not chemistry — and if AI-discovered magnets ever reach production scale, the leverage China currently holds over rare earths becomes a chemistry problem that someone has already solved, which is a very different kind of AI risk story than the one everyone is arguing about.

Gartner: $2.59 trillion of AI spend this year — and 45% of it is plumbing

The headline number is enormous; the composition is the actual story. Gartner puts worldwide AI spending at $2.59 trillion in 2026, up 47% year-on-year — but more than 45% of that goes to infrastructure: AI-optimised servers, network fabric, processing semiconductors and IaaS. Nearly half the AI boom is, in accounting terms, real estate and electricity.

  • Worldwide AI spend: $2.59 trillion in 2026, up 47%.
  • Over 45% of it is AI infrastructure, not software.
  • Covers AI-optimised IaaS, servers, network fabric, semiconductors.
  • Gartner's January estimate was $2.5tn — it has been revised up since.
Why it matters:If nearly half of AI spending is capex on servers and fabric, then the "AI market" is mostly a hardware cycle wearing a software costume — and hardware cycles turn, which is why the software and services layer is racing to attach itself to something recurring before the build-out plateaus.

The $2.5tn vs $487bn trap — the most common budgeting error of the year

Two respectable analyst numbers, wildly different, and people keep putting them in the same slide. Gartner's headline counts total AI spending — infrastructure, services, software, devices — while IDC's $487bn figure for 2026 measures AI infrastructure hardware only, which means the two forecasts are not alternatives to each other and mixing them is, as one analysis puts it, the single most common analytical error in technology budgeting. Read the denominator before you quote the number.

  • Gartner: $2.59tn — the whole AI stack, end to end.
  • IDC: $487bn — AI infrastructure hardware only for 2026.
  • The forecasts measure fundamentally different things.
  • Mixing them is flagged as the year's most common budgeting error.
Why it matters:Every board deck this quarter will cite one of these numbers to justify a budget — and the person who knows which scope each figure covers wins the argument, because a 5x discrepancy in a headline is usually a definitional gap, not a disagreement about the future.

AI servers to triple in five years — and become the biggest line of all

They're building for demand that is forecast, not booked.

  • AI-optimised server spend to triple over five years.

  • The anticipated workloads: GenAI models and agentic workflows.
Why it matters:"Expanding capacity in anticipation" is the phrase that should give you pause — it's a supply-led boom, and supply-led booms only stay rational if the agentic workloads actually show up, which is exactly the thing Forrester keeps finding enterprises haven't deployed yet.
