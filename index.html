Six adjournments and teargas — Parliament's session opens in chaos

The Lok Sabha was adjourned a sixth time on day one as Opposition MPs shouted down proceedings, and outside the building police fired teargas as protesters under the "Cockroach Janata Party" banner tried to march on Parliament despite Delhi Police denying permission and imposing prohibitory orders across New Delhi. Inside and outside, day one was a standoff.

  • Lok Sabha adjourned a sixth time amid continuous Opposition protest.
  • Police fired teargas as CJP protesters marched toward Parliament.
  • Delhi Police denied march permission, imposed prohibitory orders.
  • Congress's Manickam Tagore filed an adjournment notice on the NEET-UG leak.
Why it matters:Six adjournments before the session has really begun isn't just noise — it signals the Opposition intends to run the clock on the government's legislative calendar, which raises the political price of every bill the Centre wants to push through in the next three weeks and turns floor management into the real contest.

Five new bills — with the FCRA amendment as the one to watch

Behind the shouting is a real legislative agenda, and one item in it will do more than the rest. The Centre is expected to table five new bills this session, and the standout is an amendment to the Foreign Contribution Regulation Act — the law that governs how NGOs and civil-society groups can receive money from abroad — alongside Amit Shah's Prevention of Insults to National Honour (Amendment) Bill, which he's set to introduce in the Rajya Sabha. The FCRA line is where policy meets pressure.

  • Five new bills expected across the session.
  • The FCRA amendment tightens rules on foreign funding of NGOs.
  • Shah to introduce the Prevention of Insults to National Honour Bill.
  • A separate bill would raise Supreme Court judges from 33 to 37.
Why it matters:FCRA is the quiet lever that decides which civil-society organisations can keep operating at scale — tightening it rarely makes headlines the way a marquee bill does, but it reshapes who gets to fund advocacy in India, which is exactly why it's slotted into a noisy session where it draws less scrutiny.

The Supreme Court is about to get four more judges

A small-sounding bill with outsized consequences for how fast Indian justice moves. Union Minister Arjun Ram Meghwal moved to introduce the Supreme Court (Number of Judges) Amendment Bill, 2026, which raises the court's sanctioned strength from 33 judges to 37 — the first increase in years, aimed squarely at a pendency backlog that runs into tens of thousands of cases. More benches, in theory, means more disposals.

  • Bill raises Supreme Court strength from 33 to 37 judges.
  • Introduced by Law Minister Arjun Ram Meghwal.
  • Targets a massive case-pendency backlog.
  • More judges enables more constitution and division benches.
Why it matters:Adding judges is the least glamorous judicial reform and often the most effective — capacity, not doctrine, is what clears backlogs, so four extra seats on the top court can shift how quickly the biggest constitutional questions actually get decided, which matters more than any single ruling.

  • Trails only Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 on overall capability.
  • 1M-token context, native images; full open weights July 27.
Why it matters:An open model this close to the frontier resets the strategic map — enterprises wary of US-model export controls now have a near-top-tier alternative they can self-host for free, which erodes both American labs' pricing power and Washington's leverage, and it's exactly why US chip stocks wobbled on Friday even as the model itself is a bullish signal for AI demand.

DuctGPT is inventing magnets that don't need China

This is the AI story with actual geopolitical teeth. Ames Laboratory has built a physics-trained model called DuctGPT that designs rare-earth-free permanent magnets — and unlike a generic LLM, it reasons about the underlying physics while also weighing production cost and component sourcing. Permanent magnets are the choke-hold in EV motors, wind turbines and defence hardware, and China controls the rare earths that go into them.

  • DuctGPT is physics-trained, not a general-purpose LLM.
  • Targets rare-earth-free permanent magnets.
  • Optimises for production cost and component sourcing, not just performance.
  • Built at the US Department of Energy's Ames Laboratory.
Why it matters:A model that designs around a supply constraint is doing industrial policy, not chemistry — and if AI-discovered magnets ever reach production scale, the leverage China currently holds over rare earths becomes a chemistry problem that someone has already solved, which is a very different kind of AI risk story than the one everyone is arguing about.

Gartner: $2.59 trillion of AI spend this year — and 45% of it is plumbing

The headline number is enormous; the composition is the actual story. Gartner puts worldwide AI spending at $2.59 trillion in 2026, up 47% year-on-year — but more than 45% of that goes to infrastructure: AI-optimised servers, network fabric, processing semiconductors and IaaS. Nearly half the AI boom is, in accounting terms, real estate and electricity.

  • Worldwide AI spend: $2.59 trillion in 2026, up 47%.
  • Over 45% of it is AI infrastructure, not software.
  • Covers AI-optimised IaaS, servers, network fabric, semiconductors.
  • Gartner's January estimate was $2.5tn — it has been revised up since.
Why it matters:If nearly half of AI spending is capex on servers and fabric, then the "AI market" is mostly a hardware cycle wearing a software costume — and hardware cycles turn, which is why the software and services layer is racing to attach itself to something recurring before the build-out plateaus.
Gartner CIO Dive

The $2.5tn vs $487bn trap — the most common budgeting error of the year

Two respectable analyst numbers, wildly different, and people keep putting them in the same slide. Gartner's headline counts total AI spending — infrastructure, services, software, devices — while IDC's $487bn figure for 2026 measures AI infrastructure hardware only, which means the two forecasts are not alternatives to each other and mixing them is, as one analysis puts it, the single most common analytical error in technology budgeting. Read the denominator before you quote the number.

  • Gartner: $2.59tn — the whole AI stack, end to end.
  • IDC: $487bn — AI infrastructure hardware only for 2026.
  • The forecasts measure fundamentally different things.
  • Mixing them is flagged as the year's most common budgeting error.
Why it matters:Every board deck this quarter will cite one of these numbers to justify a budget — and the person who knows which scope each figure covers wins the argument, because a 5x discrepancy in a headline is usually a definitional gap, not a disagreement about the future.

AI servers to triple in five years — and become the biggest line of all

Gartner has a specific prediction buried in the spending report, and it's a big one. Spending on AI-optimised servers is set to triple over the next five years to become the largest single subsegment of AI spending, as cloud providers build capacity in anticipation of workloads that GenAI models and agentic workflows haven't actually generated yet. They're building for demand that is forecast, not booked.

  • AI-optimised server spend to triple over five years.
  • It becomes the largest subsegment of all AI spending.
  • Driven by cloud providers expanding capacity ahead of demand.
  • The anticipated workloads: GenAI models and agentic workflows.
Why it matters:"Expanding capacity in anticipation" is the phrase that should give you pause — it's a supply-led boom, and supply-led booms only stay rational if the agentic workloads actually show up, which is exactly the thing Forrester keeps finding enterprises haven't deployed yet.
